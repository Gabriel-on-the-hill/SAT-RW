🌍 The Foundation — Independent vs Dependent Clauses

Before any punctuation rule makes sense, you must be able to identify clause type. Every Boundaries question depends on this.

✓ Independent Clause
A complete sentence. Has a subject and a verb. Can stand alone.
"The scientist conducted the experiment."
"The results were surprising."
⊖ Dependent Clause
Has a subject and verb but CANNOT stand alone. Usually starts with a subordinating word.
"Although the scientist conducted the experiment…"
"Because the results were surprising…"
🔮 The Clause Test (your most important tool)
Read the clause by itself. Ask: "Could this stand alone as a sentence?"
YES → Independent clause  ·  NO → Dependent clause

📍 The Decision Flowchart — every Boundaries question

Walk through these questions in order. The first YES tells you which punctuation to use.

QuestionIf YES → answer
Are both parts independent clauses? Use semicolon OR comma + FANBOYS OR period
Does the second part explain / illustrate the first (and part 1 is independent)? Use colon
Is this extra info you could remove without breaking the sentence? Use a pair of dashes / commas / parens
Does a dependent clause / introductory phrase come first? Use a comma after the dependent clause
None of the above? Use no punctuation

FANBOYS = For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So — the seven coordinating conjunctions that work with a comma to join two independent clauses.
