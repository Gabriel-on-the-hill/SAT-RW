💡 How to use this page

Information & Ideas looks like four separate skills. It is really two habits — the Coverage Test and R/S/D / G/B/C — applied to four question formats.

The first move on every question: read the stem → name the sub-skill → apply that technique. If you start reading the passage without knowing which sub-skill you are in, you will read it wrong.

Tap a card or a tab above to jump straight to the sub-skill.

💡 The hidden connection

Three of the four sub-skills are the same instinct in a different form:

  • Coverage Test on Central Ideas → Sufficiency on Evidence → Bounded + Complete on Inferences. All three ask: does this answer cover everything the passage gives me — no more, no less?
  • R/S/D works identically whether the evidence is a quote (Textual) or a data point (Quantitative). Learn it once, apply it twice.

Get comfortable with the Coverage Test and R/S/D, and you've covered most of Information & Ideas already.

2.1

Central Ideas — The Claim Hierarchy

Coverage Test · ~8–10 Qs/test
🔍 Trigger — recognize the stem
  • "Which choice best states the main idea of the text?"
  • "According to the text, what is true about [topic]?"
  • "The text's central claim is that…"
  • "Based on the text, what does the researcher conclude?"

Key distinction from Part 1 (Text Structure): you are not being asked what the passage does (argues, explains, challenges). You are being asked what the passage says.

📐 Technique — The Claim Hierarchy

Every passage has a three-level hierarchy. Wrong answers come from the middle and bottom — they are true, just too narrow.

CENTRAL CLAIM ← broadest assertion, covers all details ↓ SUPPORTING DETAILS ← sub-points, evidence categories ↓ EXAMPLES / EVIDENCE ← names, dates, specific studies

The Coverage Test — the most important move:

"Does this cover EVERYTHING the passage discusses?"

If the passage discusses health and environment and social benefits, an answer about only health is too narrow — even though it is true.

🎯 Decision tree
Read answer choice ↓ Q1: Is this stated in the passage? NO → eliminate ↓ YES Q2: Does it cover EVERYTHING? NO → too narrow ↓ YES Q3: Is it the BROADEST accurate statement? NO → keep looking ↓ YES SELECT
⚠️ The three traps
Trap 1 — Detail-Pick
Picking a supporting detail that's true but only covers one part of the passage. Student reads about urban green spaces, sees one paragraph on health, picks "Green spaces improve health" — ignoring environmental and social paragraphs.
Counter: apply the Coverage Test. The central claim must encompass every point the passage made.
Trap 2 — Topic ≠ Claim
Picking "The text is about photosynthesis." That's the topic, not the claim. A claim asserts something about the topic.
Counter: the claim must contain an action verb (is / does / shows / suggests / demonstrates). "Photosynthesis" is a topic; "Photosynthesis is more efficient in shade plants" is a claim.
Trap 3 — Overgeneralization
Stretching beyond what the passage actually says. Passage discusses three cities — answer says "all cities worldwide."
Counter: watch for all / every / always / never. If passage shows some, answer must say some.

DifficultyTimeAccuracy goal
Easy 40–50 s 90%+
Medium 50–60 s 80–85%
Hard 60–70 s 75–80%
2.2

Command of Evidence — Textual

R/S/D · ~6–8 Qs/test
🔍 Trigger — recognize the stem
  • "Which quotation from the text best supports the claim that [X]?"
  • "Which finding from the study, if true, would most directly support the researcher's claim?"
  • "Which statement from the passage provides the best evidence for [claim]?"
  • "The author claims that [X]. Which choice best illustrates this claim?"

Key distinction from Central Ideas: in 2.1 you identified the claim. In 2.2 the claim is given to you — your job is to evaluate evidence for it.

📐 Technique — R/S/D

Three criteria, in order. Every quote must pass all three.

R
Relevance
Does the evidence address the specific claim?
S
Sufficiency
Does it cover ALL parts of the claim?
D
Directness
Does it explicitly support — not just relate to — the claim?
🎯 Decision framework
Break the claim into components ↓ For each quote: Q1: RELEVANT to the claim? NO → eliminate ↓ YES Q2: SUFFICIENT (covers all parts)? NO → partial support, eliminate ↓ YES Q3: DIRECT (explicit, not related)? NO → topical only, eliminate ↓ YES SELECT
⚠️ The three traps
Trap 1 — Partial Support
Claim says "X improves both efficiency and accuracy." Evidence shows only efficiency — accurate but incomplete.
Counter: split multi-part claims into components. Every component must be addressed.
Trap 2 — Topically Related, Not Direct
Claim: "Solar is cost-competitive with fossil fuels." Quote: "Solar panel efficiency has improved significantly." Same topic, doesn't prove cost.
Counter: ask "does this prove the claim, or just discuss the same topic?" Topical relevance ≠ evidential support.
Trap 3 — Methodology Instead of Finding
Claim: "The study found meditation reduces stress." Quote: "Researchers recruited 200 participants." That's how, not what was found.
Counter: if the claim describes a finding, the evidence must state the finding — not the methodology or context.
⏱️ Time targets
DifficultyTimeAccuracy goal
Easy 50–60 s 90%+
Medium 60–70 s 80–85%
Hard 70–75 s 75–80%
2.3

Command of Evidence — Quantitative

Data Read + R/S/D · ~4–6 Qs/test
🔍 Trigger — recognize the stem
  • "Which choice most effectively uses data from the graph to support the claim?"
  • "Which statement from the table best illustrates the researcher's conclusion that [X]?"
  • "The text claims [X]. Which data point from the chart provides the strongest support?"
  • "Based on the figure, which statement about [topic] is most accurate?"

Key distinction from Evidence-Textual: the evidence is now a graph or table instead of a quote. R/S/D is identical — but you must read the visual correctly before applying it.

📐 Step 1 — Read the visual systematically

Do this before looking at the answer choices.

ElementWhat to check
TitleWhat is this graph/table showing?
Axes / HeadersWhat does each axis or column represent?
UnitsThousands? Millions? Percentages? Raw numbers? Highlight this.
ScaleDoes the y-axis start at zero? Are intervals regular?
Data PointsIdentify specific values, trends, patterns relevant to the claim

Reading "250" on an axis labeled Population (in thousands) and writing "250 people" is a 1000× magnitude error. Always: Read value → Apply unit → State complete number.

📐 Step 2 — Apply R/S/D

Identical to Evidence-Textual — every data point must pass all three.

R
Relevance
Does this data address the specific claim?
S
Sufficiency
Does it cover all components — including correct time period and category?
D
Directness
Does the data EXPLICITLY show what the claim asserts?
⚠️ The three traps
Trap 1 — Unit / Scale Misread
Reading "250" on a thousands-labeled axis as "250 people" instead of 250,000. A 1000× magnitude error.
Counter: circle units immediately after reading axes. Read → Apply unit → State complete number.
Trap 2 — Correlation ≠ Causation
Two trends rise together (ice cream sales, drownings) — answer says "X causes Y." Both are actually caused by a third factor (warm weather).
Counter: data shows correlation / association / trend — not causes / leads to / results in unless the study explicitly tested causation.
Trap 3 — Wrong Time / Wrong Category
Claim: "between 2015 and 2020" — data shows 2010–2015. Or claim is about "teenagers" — data is about "young adults."
Counter: circle time range and population in both the claim and the answer. Scope must match precisely.

DifficultyTimeAccuracy goal
Easy 60–70 s 90%+
Medium 70–80 s 80–85%
Hard 80–90 s 75–80%
2.4

Inferences — The Validity Test

G/B/C · ~6–8 Qs/test
🔍 Trigger — recognize the stem
  • "Based on the text, which statement about [topic] is most likely true?"
  • "Which conclusion about [X] is best supported by the passage?"
  • "The text most strongly suggests that…"
  • "It can reasonably be inferred from the passage that…"
  • "Which statement best describes what the passage implies about [topic]?"

Key distinction: the correct answer is not stated explicitly but is logically supported by what is stated. You are drawing a conclusion, not identifying one.

💡 The Pre-Check (do this first)

Before applying G/B/C, run a fast filter on every choice:

"Is this choice directly stated in the passage?"
If YES → almost always wrong on inference questions.

You were asked to infer, not to identify. This pre-check alone eliminates 25–40% of wrong answers on Inference Hard.

📐 Technique — The Inference Validity Test
G
Grounded
Does this rest on actual passage content (not outside knowledge)?
B
Bounded
Does this stay within reasonable limits (not overgeneral, not too specific)?
C
Complete
Does this account for ALL relevant info (not cherry-picking)?
🎯 Decision framework
Read passage. Note key facts, claims, evidence. ↓ For each answer choice: Q1: Is this STATED in the passage? YES → probably wrong ↓ NO Q2: GROUNDED (based on passage content)? NO → outside knowledge, eliminate ↓ YES Q3: BOUNDED (reasonable scope)? NO → too extreme or trivial ↓ YES Q4: COMPLETE (considers all relevant info)? NO → ignores contradictions ↓ YES SELECT
⚠️ The three traps
Trap 1 — True But Ungrounded
Choice sounds reasonable and may be factually true — but the passage didn't say it. "Endangered species have complex breeding requirements" when passage discussed only habitat loss.
Counter: for every inference, point to specific passage text that supports it. If you can't, eliminate — even if it's true in general.
Trap 2 — Overgeneralization
"200 college students showed X" → answer says "All students show X." Leaps beyond what the evidence supports.
Counter: circle limiting words (200 / college / correlated). Does the answer respect those limits? Some college students — valid. All students — overgeneralizes.
Trap 3 — Cherry-Picked
Passage shows a method succeeded in wealthy schools but struggled in under-resourced ones. Answer: "The method is highly effective" — ignores the struggle.
Counter: ask "does the passage present any contradictions, limitations, or nuances?" A complete inference must acknowledge them.
⏱️ Time targets
DifficultyTimeAccuracy goal
Easy 50–60 s 90%+
Medium 60–70 s 80–85%
Hard 70–80 s 75–80%

⏱️ Time-pressure decision rules

When you have less than the target time on a question:

  • Central Ideas (≤30 s left): read first + last sentences only. Pick the broader if they conflict. Eliminate anything with a limiting phrase the passage didn't use.
  • Evidence · Text (≤30 s left): run only the D check. Pick the quote that most explicitly contains a phrase from the claim.
  • Evidence · Quant (≤45 s left): trust the units check above all. Eliminate any answer whose magnitude is impossible given the visual's scale.
  • Inferences (≤30 s left): apply only Pre-check + Bounded. Eliminate anything stated in the passage; eliminate anything containing all / every / always / never / proves.
  • If you must guess: on CI / Inf, prefer the least extreme answer. On Evidence, prefer the choice whose noun phrases most overlap with the claim's.
📚 Micro-glossary — easily-confused stem words

These words appear in question stems and answer choices. Misreading them costs accuracy on the hardest questions.

WordWhat it meansTrap it creates
Suggests / ImpliesAn inference invitation. Answer is NOT stated.Picking an answer that is stated — that's identification, not inference
IllustratesProvides a concrete example of an already-stated claimPicking an answer that makes a claim instead of exemplifying one
DemonstratesShows/proves a claim through evidencePicking an answer that merely mentions the topic
SupportsProvides evidence forPicking an answer that describes or defines without proof
IndicatesPoints toward — weaker than provesPicking an answer that overstates (uses proves / establishes)

Tip: if the stem says suggests or implies, treat it as an Inference question even if it doesn't say "infer."

🥇 One-screen cheat sheet
1. Read the stem first. It tells you which of the 4 sub-skills you are in.
2. Central Ideas = Claim Hierarchy + Coverage Test. Pick the broadest answer still in the passage.
3. Evidence (Text or Graph) = R/S/D. Relevant. Sufficient. Direct. In that order.
4. Quantitative = read units FIRST. Then R/S/D, with extra attention to time and category.
5. Inference = pre-check then G/B/C. Stated → wrong. Unground → wrong. Too broad → wrong. Cherry-picks → wrong.
6. Watch all / every / always / never / proves / causes. Almost always traps.
7. The criteria are the answer. The quote / data / inference is just confirmation.
